The discipline of the previous chapters applies here. Providence is not an AI alignment project. It does not train models, develop safety techniques, or contribute to the technical alignment literature. It does not claim to solve the problem of intelligence in right relationship.

Providence is being designed to operate on the substrate within which AI will increasingly operate. The relational coordination layer described in Chapter Five exists because coherent deployment of intelligence at scale depends on coherent communities, institutions, and feedback architectures. Whether AI is eventually deployed in service of regeneration depends on whether the people deploying it are embedded in relational fields that perceive what regeneration requires. Providence is one attempt to strengthen that substrate. It does not build AI. It is being designed to build some conditions under which AI built elsewhere might be turned toward purposes the current substrate makes difficult.

One more specific research question deserves naming, not a product commitment: should AI systems eventually be integrated into Providence’s coordination infrastructure to map alignment between communities, surface latent collaboration, model the regenerative impact of proposed work, or support deliberation across federated networks? The case for integration is real. Civilization is becoming too complex for unaided human coordination, and AI that helps people perceive interdependence rather than fragmenting attention could be genuinely useful. The case against is equally real. AI integrated into a coordination platform handling values, intentions, and trust signals creates exactly the kind of architecture careful readers should regard skeptically, regardless of intent. This design question remains unresolved, will be developed openly, and will be subject to the discipline governing every architectural decision in the project: refusing designs that begin to reproduce the dynamics the project was built to refuse.
