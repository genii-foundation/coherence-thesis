The deepest claim of this chapter should be evaluated on its own terms, separate from any specific project. Civilization has built extraordinary instruments for moving value along the pathways extraction recognizes and almost none for moving it along the pathways regeneration requires. This asymmetry is not natural. It follows from centuries of design decisions that privileged what could be efficiently measured and transferred over what sustained the conditions of measurement and transfer.

A civilization wielding artificial intelligence and exponentially powerful technology while operating through this asymmetry is unlikely to remain stable. The reason is structural rather than moral. AI-amplified extraction-accounting systems will optimize extraction more efficiently. The foundations supporting civilization will be consumed faster, and the conditions for any coordination will degrade faster than infrastructure can adapt. The technology does not create the problem. It accelerates a problem the accounting was already producing.

Building parallel currencies, regenerative ownership structures, and coordination infrastructures that perceive what extraction cannot is therefore a central design task of the present moment. Work is already happening at the edges, in Sardex, Mondragón, and countless smaller experiments, mostly invisible to mainstream economic discourse. Whether these experiments connect, learn from one another’s failures, and develop the cumulative sophistication needed to operate at civilizational scale is an open question for the coming decades.

The answer is not yet known. The work is underway in fragments. The question itself can no longer be deferred. Money will continue to do what it does. The remaining question is what else civilization is willing to build alongside it, and whether those structures can sustain the conditions money was never designed to perceive.

*The next chapter turns to the governance dimension of the same question: how decisions are made about systems shaping collective life, and whether forms of deliberation more coherent than the procedures currently available can address modern governance’s legitimacy crisis.*
