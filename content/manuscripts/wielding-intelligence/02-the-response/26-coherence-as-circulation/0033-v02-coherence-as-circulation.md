This reframes what coherence might mean in an economic context. Earlier in this book, coherence was described as something like capital. That framing has problems. Capital is an instrument of accumulation. Coherence does not accumulate well. It degrades when stockpiled, the way trust does, the way relational depth does, the way attention does. Coherence is more accurately understood as a property of circulation. It exists in the patterns by which value moves through a system, not in the units the system counts.

A healthy economy circulates value among the conditions that sustain it. A regenerative agricultural economy returns value to the soil that produces the food, the laborers who tend it, the communities whose health depends on it, and the ecological systems whose regulation depends on agriculture. An extractive agricultural economy moves value outward, away from the soil, away from the laborers, away from the local community, toward distant shareholders and financial intermediaries. The total wealth generated may be similar in the short term. The pattern of flow differs entirely, and over time it produces entirely different outcomes.

Structurally, this is what coherence as flow means. It is not a score attached to an individual. It is a property visible in the architecture of how value moves through a system over time. Whether a community is regenerating or depleting itself can be observed through the circulation patterns of its financial, social, ecological, and relational currencies. Those patterns are visible without intimate surveillance of any participant.

This changes the instruments needed. The question is no longer, “How do we measure individual coherence and reward it?” It is, “What coordination infrastructures support circulation patterns that regenerate the conditions communities depend on?” These are different design problems, and the second is more tractable than the first.
