Many people are not sleeping well. The reason is not always obvious to them. Their lives may be functional. Their relationships may be intact. Their work may be meaningful. And yet something operates beneath the surface: background dread, structural unease, a persistent sense that the ground is shifting in ways no individual response can address. This is not pathology. It is accurate perception.

People in their twenties are inheriting a civilization that visibly does not work while being told to build careers within it. The institutions they were raised to trust, universities, governments, professional fields, and news organizations, have lost much of their legitimacy as they came of age. The economic systems they are entering offer worse prospects than their parents had at the same age, often substantially worse. The ecological conditions they will live under longest are deteriorating at rates older generations have not had to account for in their own planning. The technological conditions they will work within are being designed with little input from those whose careers will be most affected. They must do all of this while being asked to demonstrate that they are resilient, optimistic, and well adjusted.

People in their thirties and forties are often the load-bearing generation of a moment they did not design. They run institutions whose foundations are crumbling. They raise children into conditions they cannot promise. They work inside organizations whose stated values often contradict their operational logics, and they see the contradiction in ways earlier generations of professionals did not. Many carry private grief for the world they were taught to expect and the one they have learned exists. Many do this without permission to name it.

People in their fifties and sixties are watching systems they spent their working lives inside transform in ways that often invalidate accumulated expertise. The careers they built have been disrupted, often more than once. Their expected retirements look different. Many are watching parents die in increasingly impersonal healthcare systems and wondering whether they will receive the care they hoped to give their own parents. They can also see the longer arc of change in civilizational systems across their lifetimes, and the perspective is often more disturbing than current discourse acknowledges.

People older than this are watching the world they helped build become unrecognizable. Some carry guilt for what their generation did or failed to do. Some grieve what is being lost. Some have a stoic acceptance born of surviving more than one era of disruption. Many watch grandchildren inherit conditions they do not know how to help them prepare for.

These are not separate experiences. They are different positions within the same threshold.
