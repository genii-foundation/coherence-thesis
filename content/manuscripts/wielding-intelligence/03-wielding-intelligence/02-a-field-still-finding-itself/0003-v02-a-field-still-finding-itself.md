If a careful researcher mapped the work the previous chapters have been pointing toward, they would not find a coordinated movement. They would find dozens of distinct lineages, mostly unaware of one another, working in adjacent design spaces with overlapping vocabularies and little coordination.

In one cluster, the regenerative-coordination world has been building infrastructure for commons-based coordination, regenerative finance, bioregional governance, and post-extractive economic experimentation. OpenCivics has run a Network Coordination Commons since 2024, convening coordinators from civic-innovation projects to share what they have learned about how networks work. The Capital Institute’s Regenerative Communities Network connects place-based regenerative initiatives across multiple continents. ReFi DAO coordinates communities working on regenerative finance through cryptographic infrastructure. The Funding the Commons gatherings, the Crypto Commons Gathering in Europe, the Earth Regeneration Alliance, and a growing constellation of bioregional networks approach the same underlying question from different angles: how can value circulate among aligned actors without being captured by extractive incentives?

In another cluster, what this book calls the inner-development world has been building parallel infrastructure for the physiological and contemplative substrate described in the diagnostic chapters. The Inner Development Goals framework, established by a Swedish nonprofit in 2020 and now integrated into curricula, leadership programs, and policy frameworks across multiple countries, offers a structured language for the inner capacities sustainable transformation requires. The Contemplative Sustainable Futures Program at Lund University, the Mind & Life Institute, the Garrison Institute, and the Initiative for Contemplation, Equity and Action each work at a different layer of the question of how human beings develop the capacities complex civilization depends on. The 2025 IDG Summit gathered researchers, practitioners, and policymakers from over fifty countries to share what is being learned about how inner development catalyzes outer change.

These two worlds rarely overlap. The regenerative-coordination world tends to be technologically literate and oriented toward design and finance, often skeptical of what reads as soft. The inner-development world tends to be relationally literate and oriented toward practice and psychology, often skeptical of what reads as extractive. Each has something the other needs. Both are addressing the same civilizational problem from different sides.

A third, broader cluster includes work the book has already named: AI alignment research, deliberation platforms like Pol.is and vTaiwan, regenerative agriculture practitioners, Indigenous-led conservation initiatives, ecological-restoration networks, restorative-justice systems, mutual-credit experiments like Sardex, and cooperative federations like Mondragón. Each does work the others depend on, and most operate in relative isolation from the rest.

This is what the book has been describing as the unfinished field. The work exists. The people doing it exist. What does not yet exist is the coordination infrastructure that would let them find each other at the density the present moment requires.
