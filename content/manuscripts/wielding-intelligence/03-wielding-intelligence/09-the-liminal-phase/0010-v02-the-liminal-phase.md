Anthropology has a name for this. The liminal phase, in the work of Arnold van Gennep and later Victor Turner, is the middle phase of a transition, the period between the leaving of one structure and the arrival of another. In ritual contexts, the liminal phase is structured: there is an ending, a passage, and an arrival, and the community holds the person passing through. In civilizational contexts, the liminal phase often arrives without structure. The old has not finished ending. The new has not yet begun. The person living through it has no map.

This is the phase humanity appears to be in. The civilization that organized roughly the last several centuries, built on extractive economic logic, nation-state governance, a mechanistic worldview, and the separation of human beings from the living systems they depend on, is visibly failing to metabolize the complexity it has produced. What comes after has not yet stabilized into recognizable form. Much of this book has been describing what could come, what is beginning to come, what would have to be built. But none of that has yet arrived as the dominant condition. The dominant condition, for most people, is the liminal phase itself.

The liminal phase has a particular psychological signature. People in liminal periods often feel destabilized in ways that do not map cleanly onto specific causes. They feel exhausted by efforts that previously sustained them. They feel uncertain about commitments that previously felt clear. They feel an anticipatory grief whose object they cannot quite name. They feel, sometimes, that they are losing the capacity to imagine the future they used to be able to imagine. These are accurate responses to the structural condition of living in between civilizational logics.

The risk of the liminal phase is that it can produce, in those experiencing it, either premature foreclosure or extended paralysis. Premature foreclosure is the impulse to grasp at whatever comes along that promises stability: authoritarian certainty, ideological purity, technological salvation, withdrawal into private life. Extended paralysis is the inability to act because the conditions for meaningful action are not yet clear. Both are responses to the same underlying condition: the absence of structure that holds the passage.

What helps in liminal phases, when help exists, is the presence of others passing through the same threshold and the existence of practices and communities that recognize what is happening as a passage rather than as personal failure. The book has been describing this infrastructure in different vocabulary. The relational coordination layer, the trust architecture, the communities of practice that have been quietly developing somatic and contemplative capacities, the experiments in regenerative economics and coherent governance: each of these can be understood as the beginning of a civilizational holding for a passage that civilization has not previously had to hold.
