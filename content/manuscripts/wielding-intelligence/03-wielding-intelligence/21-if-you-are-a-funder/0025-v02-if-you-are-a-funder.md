If you can direct capital and the book’s argument has changed how you think about where it should go, the request is straightforward. The coherence infrastructure the book describes is systematically undercapitalized relative to simultaneous capability development. In any given year, capital flowing into AI capability development exceeds, by orders of magnitude, the total flowing into the coordination, contemplative, governance, and regenerative-economics work the book describes.

This is not natural. It follows from decisions about where capital goes and what returns are considered legitimate. It can change. Change requires funders who can hold time horizons longer than venture capital typically permits, accept that returns on coherence infrastructure are largely non-financial, and resist institutional pressure to direct philanthropic and impact capital toward projects that are easiest to measure and least likely to challenge the systems that produced the capital.

If this is your work, find the builders named in the previous section and direct capital toward them on terms they can accept. This sometimes means accepting forms of accountability and return that conventional investment vehicles do not permit, funding projects whose outcomes you cannot predict, and trusting builders whose judgment about deployment you have come to respect more than your own. This coordination infrastructure needs patient, structurally aligned capital, and very little is available to builders who would otherwise do the work.
