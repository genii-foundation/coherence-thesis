If you are in a position to direct capital and the book’s argument has changed how you think about where capital should go, the request is straightforward. The coherence infrastructure the book has described is systematically undercapitalized relative to the capability development happening at the same time. Chapter Eleven gave the scale of the asymmetry.

That asymmetry follows from decisions about where capital has been directed and what kinds of returns have been considered legitimate, and what was decided can be decided differently. The change requires funders who can hold longer time horizons than venture capital typically permits and who can accept that the returns on coherence infrastructure are largely non-financial. It also asks them to resist the institutional pressures that direct most philanthropic and impact capital toward the kinds of projects that are easiest to measure and least likely to challenge the systems that produced the capital in the first place.

If this is your work, the request is to find the builders the previous section named and direct capital toward them on terms they can actually accept. This sometimes means accepting structures of accountability and return that conventional investment vehicles do not permit, or funding projects whose specific outcomes you cannot predict. Sometimes it means trusting builders whose judgment you have come to respect more than your own about how the capital should be deployed. The coordination infrastructure being built needs patient, structurally aligned capital, and very little of that capital reaches the builders who would otherwise do the work.
