A reader who has made it this far has been changed by the book, or has refused to be. Both are legitimate responses. The book was not written to manipulate readers into agreement but to articulate, as clearly as possible, conditions the reader is already living inside and a set of responses to those conditions that the reader can evaluate on their own terms.

If the book has done its work, the reader now has language for things they already perceived: the destabilization operating beneath the surface of their daily life, the fragmentation they have been navigating across institutional, ecological, relational, and informational dimensions, the work they can already feel being asked of them, the threshold they have been crossing.

What comes next is not the book’s to decide. It is the reader’s.

The Final Note that follows the next chapter makes the specific invitation as direct as the book can make it. The book has named a project called Providence and has positioned itself partly as that project’s founding document. Honesty about what the project is, what it is not, and what it has not yet figured out has been a discipline throughout. The next chapter takes that discipline to its conclusion: the most direct accounting the book is capable of making about the choices the book itself has made.
