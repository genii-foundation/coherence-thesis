The phrase *the birds and the bees* once offered a gentle euphemism for teaching children about sex and reproduction. The book returns to that second meaning because markets now mediate desire as aggressively as they mediate attention. The ecological and intimate records are not one causal story, but they share a question: what is lost when relationship becomes product?

Pornography, dating applications, and paid intimate media have changed how many people encounter sex and courtship. These forms can involve agency, livelihood, pleasure, exploitation, compulsion, loneliness, or several at once. The manuscript should not conscript every participant into one diagnosis. It criticizes businesses that profit when people keep watching or swiping while promising satisfaction or relationship.

The cited General Social Survey analysis reports that weekly sexual activity among U.S. adults fell from about **55% in 1990 to 37% by 2024**, while year-long sexlessness among young adults roughly doubled after 2010 to about one in four. It also reports a decline in in-person time with friends. These trends sit beside falling fertility and widespread loneliness, but they do not share one established cause. They describe changing conditions for intimacy that deserve inquiry without panic or shame.

Freedom from coercive marriage, gender roles, and sexual expectation was hard-won and must be protected. Solitude and childlessness are not civilizational failures. The concern is whether people have the material time, safety, trust, and community required to choose intimacy freely. Reverence can be practiced in sexual and nonsexual bonds alike. Some goods depend on relationship and are damaged when every encounter is forced through a market.

This is why Intimacy appears among the Seven Initiates. Cultural restoration and the capacity for trusting relation affect one another, but neither can be reduced to the other. Volume VII develops the proposed practice and the safeguards it requires.
