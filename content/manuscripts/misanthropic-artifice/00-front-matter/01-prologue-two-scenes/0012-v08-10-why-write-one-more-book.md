Books already exist on every thread this volume has touched: birds, soil, surveillance, water, diet, genomes, labor, war, and despair. This volume offers only a compressed account. Its contribution must therefore be coordination, not the pretense that summary replaces those literatures.

The manuscript cannot certify its own restraint. That judgment belongs to the source audit and to qualified reviewers. The strongest version of the argument will keep only figures that survive that process.

Buckminster Fuller argued that design and coordination could answer forms of scarcity, while Fritjof Capra described a turn from mechanistic toward ecological and systemic thought. Their exact claims and quotations require verification. They belong to a larger lineage of diagnosis and proposed alternatives that this volume should acknowledge without crowning any thinker as the voice of an age.

The Coherence Thesis argues that diagnosis often lacks a coordination structure through which strangers can find, trust, and provision one another. Providence, PURPOSEFUL, and ICONS are proposals for that missing structure. They are not yet a demonstrated answer, and their detail must not be mistaken for implementation.

Readers may reject this model without forfeiting moral seriousness. The proper demand is narrower: test the diagnosis, challenge the proposal, and build alternatives that answer the harms more honestly. The book now turns from indictment toward its own unfinished design.
