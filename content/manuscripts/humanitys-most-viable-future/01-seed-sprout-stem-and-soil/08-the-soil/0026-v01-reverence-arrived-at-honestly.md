Hold these observations together long enough and a kind of reverence arrives, not demanded or believed but noticed. Life appears to organize itself through relationship. Intelligence appears to emerge through participation. Cooperation appears to be at least as fundamental as competition. Systems that increase coherence tend, observably, to become more alive: more adaptive, more creative, more capable of repair. Systems that erode it tend toward brittleness and collapse.

This is reverence earned through observation rather than asserted through doctrine. It requires no belief about why life is this way, only the willingness to notice that it is, and to take the noticing seriously.

And once noticed, it suggests something radical: coherence is not only useful. It is, in some real sense, the direction in which living systems become more themselves.
