A consequential error of modern governance has been treating intelligence as something that can be centralized, outsourced, or computed independently of human condition.

In practice, intelligence is emergent. The coherent groups the Sprout described do more than outperform: they adapt more effectively, integrate feedback more reliably, and make fewer catastrophic errors under pressure.

This suggests intelligence cannot be engineered directly. It must be cultivated indirectly, by maintaining the conditions that allow it to emerge. It reframes governance not as the enforcement of outcomes, but as the protection of the capacities that make meaningful outcomes possible at all.
