Digital platforms solved a genuine discovery problem. They help people find information, communities, and work within an environment too large for any person to search unaided.

Their advertising models rewarded the conversion of attention into revenue. Engagement became a proxy for value, and emotionally arousing material often performed well under that measure. The systems learned to optimize what they could count: clicks, views, shares, and time spent. Depth of thought, quality of relationship, and the development of judgment remained harder to see.

A platform that measures attention will shape attention around its measures. Providence asks whether discovery can instead be organized around presence and demonstrated trust. That question is promising only if its instruments remain corrigible and its participants remain sovereign.

The inheritance is therefore mixed. Institutions can carry meaning, obligation, production, knowledge, and discovery beyond the reach of individual intention. They can also mistake their measures for reality, protect themselves at the expense of their purpose, and exclude what their structures cannot see.

Providence must learn from both sides. It needs temporal depth without doctrinal control, solidarity without hostile borders, operational strength without extraction, inquiry without enclosure, and discovery without capture. None of those pairings is guaranteed by good intentions. Each must appear in the design.
