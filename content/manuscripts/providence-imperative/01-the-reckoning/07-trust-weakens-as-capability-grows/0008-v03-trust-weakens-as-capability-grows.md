Large systems depend on forms of trust that rarely feel intimate. People must be able to rely on procedures, commitments, professional standards, evidence, and strangers whose work they cannot personally inspect. When that trust erodes, every actor spends more energy defending against the anticipated defection of others.

The combination matters. Greater capability among actors who trust one another less can accelerate fragmentation. Institutions hedge, competitors race, and publics interpret every request for restraint as a maneuver by someone seeking advantage. Additional capability can intensify that dynamic.

Trust cannot be restored by demanding belief. It must be earned through transparent methods, credible limits, shared governance, and repeated evidence that commitments survive pressure. Any proposal that relies on trust without specifying how it is earned has not yet become a design.
