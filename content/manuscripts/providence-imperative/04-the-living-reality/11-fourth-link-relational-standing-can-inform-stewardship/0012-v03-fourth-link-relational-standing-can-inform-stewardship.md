Some resources are already allocated partly through relational judgment. Land trusts choose stewards. Cooperatives admit members. Mutual aid networks decide where tools, labor, seed, and money should go. Communities appoint people to roles of care.

Providence proposes that participant-controlled evidence of sustained contribution could inform such decisions. It must never decide them alone. Material access requires transparent criteria, due process, multiple forms of evidence, conflict safeguards, and meaningful appeal.

A land trust might consider a person's history of stewardship alongside knowledge, references, community relationship, and a concrete plan. A cooperative might accept commitments built through participation rather than requiring every member to prepay. A mutual aid network might remember who has returned resources, taught others, or carried difficult work.

In these examples, COHERENCE acts as a supporting currency of relationship. It does not replace money, law, or democratic judgment. It makes some contributions easier to remember.
