In spring, Maren joins ten other people for three days at a farmhouse on restored land. There are no panels, slides, or rituals of professional networking. The gathering holds one question:

Where, in your work, do you feel the distance between what is and what could be?

On the second evening, the group sits with a man whose decade-long project has collapsed. Attention in the room becomes unusually quiet. Maren feels her own breathing change.

With everyone's consent, the research instruments are running. Later analysis suggests a period of increased physiological synchrony among several participants. The result is interesting, not conclusive. Shared stillness, shared task, respiration, posture, and social connection may all contribute. The participants' accounts matter alongside the data.

Maren does not need the signal to know that the gathering changed her. She later describes the moment as eleven nervous systems settling around one man's grief. The data gives the group a more precise question: what conditions allowed them to stay without trying to fix or flee it?
