Many Indigenous nations and communities have developed forms of governance grounded in reciprocal obligations to land, water, other species, and future generations. These traditions are diverse and cannot be treated as one system. Their common challenge to modern institutions is clear: human decision making does not occur outside the living world, and descendants are affected participants even when they are not present to speak.

Providence draws from this lesson with caution. Learning must not become another extraction from peoples whose lands and knowledge have already been taken. Relationship, consent, attribution, and material reciprocity are conditions of honest inheritance.
