An intimate instrument can be turned toward ends opposite its purpose. Four dangers belong at the center of the design.

**Surveillance.** Physiological and conversational data could be sold, subpoenaed, stolen, or repurposed by employers, insurers, states, or other institutions. Legal promises alone are insufficient. Raw data should remain local, export should be explicit, and the system should minimize what exists to be seized.

**Scoring.** A developmental record could harden into rank. A single number would erase context, invite competition, and make exclusion easy. The record should preserve trajectory and uncertainty, remain under participant control, and never gate basic survival, dignity, or membership.

**Measurement effects.** People may begin performing for the sensors, optimizing biomarkers instead of attending to one another. Feedback should usually arrive after the encounter. Measurement-free spaces must remain available. The network should study whether the instrument improves or degrades the quality it seeks to support.

**Bias and misreading.** Models may encode the bodies, languages, cultures, and assumptions of the populations used to train them. Every stream requires individual baselines, subgroup evaluation, accessible contest, published error rates, and the ability to refuse measurement without losing belonging.

The deepest safeguard is the willingness to discover that the central hypothesis is wrong. If physiological correlates prove too weak, unstable, or harmful for the proposed use, the measurement layer should contract or end. Providence must not keep a seductive instrument merely because the institution has built its identity around it.
