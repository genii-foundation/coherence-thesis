Providence could become a high-trust club for people already safe, wealthy, educated, and socially fluent enough to perform its preferred forms of presence. Members might exchange opportunity while calling their mutual recognition merit.

Subsidized access does not solve this by itself. The entry process, governance, research populations, language, geography, and material distribution all shape who belongs. The network should publish who enters, who leaves, who receives resources, who holds authority, and where disparities persist.

Trajectory may be fairer than comparison with a population norm, yet it still requires a model of desirable change. People most affected by poverty, trauma, disability, and exclusion must hold design authority rather than appear only as recipients of benevolence.
