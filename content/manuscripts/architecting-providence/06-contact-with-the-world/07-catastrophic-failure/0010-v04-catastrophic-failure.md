Catastrophic failure would occur if Providence reached scale while becoming extractive or coercive. The language of coherence could then conceal surveillance, hierarchy, discrimination, or harm. This risk matters because apparent success could delay recognition.

Anti-capture rules, transparency, appeal, independent audit, and practical exit may expose the drift before it becomes irreversible. They are mitigations, not guarantees.

Exit alone is insufficient when an institution controls essential services or harms people who never joined. External law, public oversight, and remedies for nonparticipants remain necessary.

The published series provides a record against which later conduct can be judged. It is evidence of stated intent, not a safeguard. Only enforceable rights, independent scrutiny, and practical remedies can protect people from an institution acting in contradiction to its texts.
