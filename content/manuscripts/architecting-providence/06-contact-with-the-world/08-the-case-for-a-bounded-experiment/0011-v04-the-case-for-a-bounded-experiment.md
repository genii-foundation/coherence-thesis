The case for trying does not depend on certainty. Existing coordination failures are serious enough to justify careful experiments in alternatives. That does not prove Providence is the best experiment, nor does it excuse harm done in its name. The burden is to make each step proportionate, reversible where possible, and open to comparison with other approaches.

A bounded experiment needs stopping rules as well as aspirations. Evidence of coercion, repeated rights violations, unmanageable financial dependence, or ecological harm should trigger independent review and the possibility of closure.

Providence is one proposed response. Other traditions and institutions may prove more effective. The relevant test is whether an approach improves coordination without imposing greater harm or unaccountable power.

The risks of action and inaction are both real. This series judges the experiment worth pursuing. Participants and affected communities retain the right to judge otherwise.
