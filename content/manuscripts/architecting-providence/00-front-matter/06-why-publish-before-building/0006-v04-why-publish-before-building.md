A fair question hangs over all of this: if the point is to build, why write volumes first? Why not begin?

An infrastructure for distributed coordination should not remain the private interpretation of its first designers. The books expose the argument, terms, safeguards, and unresolved questions to scrutiny before those ideas acquire institutional power.

Publication is not participation, and reading is not proof of a network. Books can distribute a common vocabulary and invite criticism. Actual coordination begins only when people have a durable way to find one another, decide together, and act. Any interactive or participatory layer must be judged by what the public reader can actually use, not by what the manuscript promises may exist later.

The intended sequence is simple: coordination can form a network; sustained coordination can deepen coherence; and demonstrated coherence may eventually support a currency. Every step remains conditional on evidence and consent.
