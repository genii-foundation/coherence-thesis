Four failures deserve early safeguards.

Cultic consolidation replaces inquiry with loyalty to a vision or its authors. It makes criticism feel like betrayal and blocks the learning a pilot exists to produce. Participants need protected dissent, independent review, and a credible path to remove founders from authority.

Brand capture makes reputation more important than evidence. A visible community may hide conflict or failure to protect the wider project's standing. Public reporting should therefore include adverse results and give residents control over how their lives are represented.

Exhaustion collapse concentrates labor in the most committed people until they burn out. The first community needs bounded roles, rest, compensation, backup, and succession from the start. Care cannot wait for a later phase.

Premature scaling expands the project before the architecture has faced sustained use. Pressure may come from funders, prospective members, or publicity. Growth should depend on declared evidence thresholds rather than excitement or demand alone.
