Human beings coordinate differently as groups grow. Direct trust can carry more of the work in a small cohort. Larger populations depend increasingly on roles, records, and formal process. The manuscript uses roughly one hundred and fifty as a provisional boundary for direct-trust groups, not as a biological law.

The proposed response is nesting. Direct-trust groups would remain small while protocols coordinate work among them. A bioregional Scale could therefore contain more people than any one relational group, but the success of that distinction has not been demonstrated.

Federalism, polycentric governance, subsidiarity, and cellular movement structures offer precedents for nested coordination. Providence still needs to specify membership, representation, delegation, dispute resolution, and accountability across levels.
