The first community would shape the architecture for those that follow. It therefore needs an explicit learning mandate, informed consent to the experimental character of the work, and a way to publish failures without turning residents into exhibits.

On size: the first cohort must be large enough to expose the relational design and small enough for direct acquaintance to remain plausible. This manuscript uses thirty to one hundred and fifty people as a provisional range for a direct-trust group. It does not establish a universal human threshold. A larger Scale would need nested groups, explicit interfaces among them, and evidence that the nesting preserves accountability.

On governance: the first community needs usable rules before conflict gives founders or funders an excuse to improvise authority. Those rules must include dissent, appeal, succession, amendment, and exit. Their legal and democratic adequacy still requires review.

On economics: subsidy should be visible rather than disguised as self-sufficiency. Participants need to know who provides capital, what influence follows it, which costs the community can meet, and which costs remain externalized.

On relationship to Providence: one community would be an instance, not a template. Its practices would reflect a particular place, culture, law, and founding group. Later communities should inherit evidence and questions, not a demand to reproduce local custom.
