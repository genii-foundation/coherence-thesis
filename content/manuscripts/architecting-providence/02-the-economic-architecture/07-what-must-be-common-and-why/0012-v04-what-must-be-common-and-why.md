Identity protocols should not give one operator unilateral power to admit, exclude, or revoke participants. Common standards, portable records, and replaceable service providers can reduce that power. Personal identity and data remain under the participant's rights rather than becoming common property.

Trust infrastructure also needs shared governance. Its rules should not be controlled by an actor who benefits from favorable attestations. The claims themselves remain contextual and subject to consent, correction, expiry, and appeal.

Communities can share learning from governance, repair, and ecological practice. Contributors still need attribution, privacy, consent, and the ability to withhold sensitive knowledge. A commons is not a license to appropriate experience.

Land and ecological relationships require separate legal analysis. A community may govern some resources collectively, but it cannot erase existing rights, Indigenous sovereignty, public authority, or the interests of people outside its membership by declaring a bioregion common.
