Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) identifies recurring features of durable collective governance, including defined boundaries, locally fitted rules, participation in rulemaking, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, external recognition, and nesting. These are questions to test, not a checklist that guarantees success.

David Bollier and Silke Helfrich extend commons thinking into contemporary institutions. The Internet Engineering Task Force, Linux, and Wikipedia offer distinct cases of shared infrastructure under governance. Their differences matter as much as their similarities.

Yochai Benkler, Lawrence Lessig, and scholars of open access and open science examine how distributed knowledge can resist private appropriation. Creative Commons licenses, copyleft, and open-data frameworks offer tools. None resolves governance, privacy, maintenance, or the rights of people represented in shared data.
