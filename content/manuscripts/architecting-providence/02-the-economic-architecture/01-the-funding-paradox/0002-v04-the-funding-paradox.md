*Money can enlarge a project while narrowing what it is allowed to become.*

Providence would need capital from systems it hopes to change. That does not make every wealthy donor or investor hostile. It does mean that each source of money arrives with expectations, dependencies, and power.

Legal form determines who can own, sell, dissolve, or redirect the institution. A nonprofit, cooperative, trust, or purpose-bound company distributes those powers differently. None is safe by name alone. The documents must specify control of assets, changes of purpose, conflicts of interest, and remedies after breach.

Revenue design creates another dependency. Advertising rewards attention. Venture investment usually seeks growth and an exit. Grants follow funder priorities. Membership revenue can privilege those able to pay. Each model needs a written account of whose preferences it amplifies and how the institution can refuse them.

Time matters as well. Capital that expects a quick return can force a long project to declare victory early. Funding terms should match the period required for testing, governance, and succession, with clear provisions for failure.
