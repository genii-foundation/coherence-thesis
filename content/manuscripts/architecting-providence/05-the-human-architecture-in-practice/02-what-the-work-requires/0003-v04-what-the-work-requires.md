The capacities belong to teams, not heroic individuals.

The work needs people who can connect technical choices to constitutional consequences, and people who can test philosophical claims against implementation. Neither group should dismiss the other as shallow.

It needs conflict skills: listening, boundary setting, repair, documentation, and the ability to end a relationship safely when repair is not possible. Remaining in relationship is not always the ethical outcome.

It needs ways to work under uncertainty and public criticism without converting distress into a private deficiency. Workload, role clarity, security, supervision, and time away are institutional responsibilities.

It needs economic honesty. If the work cannot pay people enough to meet housing, healthcare, care, and family obligations, the institution should name the subsidy it is extracting from workers. Sacrifice is not a screening test for commitment.
