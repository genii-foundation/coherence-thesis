The water dispute concerns a proposed increase in withdrawals from the upper catchment. It also exposes a deeper disagreement about growth, ecological risk, and whose uncertainty should govern.

In this scenario, the water question does not begin with a vote. Earlier votes settled decisions without repairing the underlying division. Participatory legitimacy does not prohibit majority rule, but it requires a process proportionate to the stakes and the rights of those affected.

Elan facilitates with Mahlinah present as mentor. The process seeks to identify the interests and ecological limits beneath the positions. Hearing both groups does not guarantee agreement.

The imagined process takes an afternoon and reaches no consensus. It produces a limited expansion with ecological thresholds and automatic reversion. Both groups remain dissatisfied. The result is plausible, not guaranteed, and the thresholds would need a real baseline, monitor, authority, and appeal.

The outcome also depends on a small group with prior trust. At three hundred people, the same process would need representation or nesting and would lose some intimacy. That tradeoff remains untested.
