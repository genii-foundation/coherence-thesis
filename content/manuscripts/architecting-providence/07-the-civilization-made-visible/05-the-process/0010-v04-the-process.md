The founders do not decide. Their knowledge of the donor and the community's needs also makes them vulnerable to the relief the money would bring. The offer enters a constitutional process with disclosed conflicts of interest and authority distributed beyond the founding group.

The process takes weeks. Participants examine each condition separately, publish reasons, hear dissent, and speak with the donor directly. An imagined procedure still needs actual rules for quorum, representation, recusal, confidentiality, affected nonmembers, and appeal.

The growth condition conflicts with the sequencing rule because the community has not completed its proof-of-life work. The governance seat would establish that money can purchase political authority, regardless of the donor's character. Public acknowledgment is less clear. The community must distinguish ordinary credit from branding that makes a shared institution appear to belong to its benefactor.
