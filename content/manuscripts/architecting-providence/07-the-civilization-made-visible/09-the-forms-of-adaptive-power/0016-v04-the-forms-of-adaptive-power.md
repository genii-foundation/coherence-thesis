Internal influence is one form. People may seek authority for private, commercial, political, or ideological ends. The response should be transparent decisions, distributed power, conflicts-of-interest rules, rotation, appeal, and evidence of conduct. Suspicion based on identity, dissent, or intuition is not a safeguard.

Dependency is another form. A supposedly compatible funder, vendor, or protocol may change terms, coordinate with other dependencies, or become indispensable. Diversification helps only when dependencies are genuinely independent and exit remains practical.

Reputation can also be manipulated. False accusations may impose real costs, but criticism may also reveal genuine harm. The response is not to label critique an attack. It is to publish evidence, protect complainants, correct errors, and let independent review distinguish allegation from finding.

Law and regulation create a final category. Some pressure may be abusive. Some may be legitimate protection for workers, neighbors, participants, or the public. Providence cannot declare itself exempt. It needs legal competence, public accountability, and a distributed design that does not hide unlawful conduct behind sovereignty language.
