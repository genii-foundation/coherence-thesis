Charles Tilly examines how movements and states alter their tactics in response to one another. The history of COINTELPRO and other surveillance programs shows that disruption is a real institutional risk in some political contexts. It does not justify treating ordinary disagreement as infiltration or designing a community around suspicion.

Organizational resilience research examines distributed detection, incident response, and memory after failure. Those concepts are more useful here than a literal immune-system analogy, which can turn people into imagined pathogens.
