No constitutional text anticipates every dispute. Interpretation therefore creates authority. Its design must say who exercises judgment, through which procedure, under what limits, and with what accountability.

The design must avoid two failures. Authority concentrated in a founder, court, or council can become unaccountable. Yet the absence of any authoritative process can leave principles inconsistent or strategically ignored.

A credible interpretive process would distribute authority, publish reasons, permit challenge and appeal, and bind interpreters to the same principles they apply. The text does not yet specify the bodies, jurisdictions, selection rules, conflicts of interest, remedies, or legal standing required to make that process real.

Local forms may vary, but the network would need common minimums for notice, participation, reasons, appeal, and protection from retaliation.
