## 1. Editorial aim

Make the manuscripts clearer, more legible, more precise, and more beautiful without replacing their intelligence or voice with a house style.

Human prose is not defined by random roughness. It is defined by attention. Each sentence should know what it is saying, why it is present, and how it belongs to the sentences around it. Elegance should arise from exact thought, proportion, rhythm, and image. It should not be sprayed on afterward.

Review every sentence. Preserve sentences that already work. Revision is not a quota.

## 2. Hierarchy of fidelity

When editorial goals conflict, use this order:

1. Preserve factual truth, quotations, citations, names, numbers, and chronology.
2. Preserve the proposition, its scope, its modality, and its logical relationship to nearby claims.
3. Preserve distinctions between evidence, inference, analogy, speculation, invitation, and aspiration.
4. Preserve deliberate image, ambiguity, rhythm, repetition, humor, tension, and emotional temperature.
5. Improve clarity and legibility.
6. Improve compression and cadence.
7. Improve surface elegance.

Never purchase elegance with a changed claim. Never purchase brevity with a missing premise. Never purchase clarity by explaining away a living image.

## 3. Punctuation standard

### Prohibited

- Em dash, Unicode `U+2014`
- En dash, Unicode `U+2013`
- Two consecutive hyphens used as punctuation

Do not replace a prohibited mark with another mark by reflex. First identify the relationship between the clauses.

- Use a period when the thoughts deserve separate emphasis.
- Use a comma when the second phrase is grammatically dependent.
- Use a colon when the second clause specifies or reveals the first.
- Use a semicolon when two independent clauses are tightly related and equal in weight.
- Use parentheses only for genuinely incidental material.
- Rewrite the syntax when punctuation is carrying a burden that belongs to structure.

Use ordinary hyphens in established compounds when grammar requires them. Write numeric and date ranges with `to`, such as `from 2018 to 2024`.

Do not silently alter an exact quotation or bibliographic title to satisfy this rule. Verify the primary source. If the published corpus must contain no prohibited glyphs, use an accurate paraphrase with attribution or obtain explicit approval for another faithful treatment. Do not change quoted wording while continuing to present it as exact.

Avoid punctuation theater. A colon should introduce something. A fragment should earn its isolation. Italics should not perform conviction that the sentence has failed to establish.

## 4. AI slop pattern catalog

These are diagnostic patterns, not automatic deletion rules. A pattern becomes a defect when it is needless, repeated, vague, falsely emphatic, or interchangeable with prose from an unrelated subject.

### 4.1 Throat clearing

Flag openings that announce importance instead of beginning the thought.

Common forms include `It is important to remember`, `It is worth noting`, `To understand this`, `At its core`, `The key point is`, and `What we must recognize is`.

Repair by stating the point directly. Keep framing only when it changes how the reader should interpret what follows.

### 4.2 Generic transitions

Flag `Furthermore`, `Moreover`, `Additionally`, `In conclusion`, `Ultimately`, `Indeed`, and similar signals when the logical relation is already obvious or not actually specified.

Repair by naming the relation: cause, contrast, consequence, example, limitation, sequence, or return. Sometimes the best transition is none.

### 4.3 False antithesis

Flag repeated constructions such as `not merely X but Y`, `not just X but Y`, `This is not X. It is Y`, and claims that invent a weak alternative so the preferred idea can defeat it.

Keep a contrast only when both sides are plausible and the distinction matters. Otherwise state the positive claim.

### 4.4 Synthetic symmetry

Flag excessive triads, mirrored clauses, repeated anaphora, identical paragraph shapes, and lists that contain three items because three sounds finished.

Parallelism can produce force and memory. It becomes synthetic when every thought marches in the same uniform.

### 4.5 Abstract noun clusters

Flag sentences crowded with words ending in forms such as `tion`, `ment`, `ness`, `ity`, `ance`, and `ence`, especially when no person or thing acts.

Repair by recovering actors, actions, objects, conditions, and consequences. Retain technical abstractions that name real distinctions.

### 4.6 Inflated significance

Flag unsupported uses of `profound`, `fundamental`, `transformative`, `revolutionary`, `unprecedented`, `crucial`, `vital`, `radical`, `deeply`, and universal language.

Make the consequence visible instead of naming its magnitude. Preserve strong terms when the argument earns them.

### 4.7 Vague grandeur

Flag phrases such as `the fabric of existence`, `the very essence of`, `a new paradigm`, `the future of humanity`, `a testament to`, and other language that could adorn almost any subject.

Replace grandeur with the particular thing, event, choice, cost, or possibility at stake.

### 4.8 Redundant restatement

Flag paragraphs that make a claim, paraphrase it, summarize the paraphrase, then end by naming the claim again. Flag chapter endings that repeatedly announce that everything changes.

Keep the strongest formulation. Use repetition only when each recurrence changes scale, meaning, rhythm, or emotional force.

### 4.9 Repetitive scaffolds

Flag recurring openings such as `This is`, `There is`, `We must`, `The result is`, `The question is`, and `It is` when they make paragraphs sound generated from a template.

Vary syntax by following the movement of thought. Do not vary merely to avoid recurrence when recurrence is deliberate.

### 4.10 Vague reference

Flag `this`, `that`, `it`, `they`, `these systems`, and `this process` when the referent is uncertain or distant.

Name the referent. If several candidates remain, raise a query rather than guessing.

### 4.11 Conceptual laundering

Flag passages that give a familiar observation a new label and then treat the label as evidence. Naming a pattern can aid thought. It does not prove the pattern, explain its cause, or establish its importance.

Separate definition, evidence, inference, and consequence.

### 4.12 Causal overreach

Flag verbs such as `causes`, `creates`, `produces`, `determines`, `requires`, and `inevitably` when the passage establishes only correlation, contribution, tendency, or possibility.

Match the verb to the evidence. Preserve uncertainty precisely rather than burying it in fog.

### 4.13 Faux precision

Flag exact numbers, time spans, taxonomies, and rankings that lack a source or a clear method. Also flag pseudo-technical labels that imply measurement where none occurred.

Verify, qualify, source, or remove.

### 4.14 Sterile hedging

Flag layers of `may`, `might`, `could`, `perhaps`, `arguably`, `in some sense`, and `to some extent` that make the sentence impossible to disagree with and equally impossible to use.

Keep uncertainty where the knowledge is uncertain. State what kind of uncertainty remains.

### 4.15 Generic uplift

Flag endings that rise automatically toward hope, invitation, collective destiny, or a better future without earning the turn through the preceding material.

Let hope arrive through a concrete possibility, decision, image, or demonstrated opening.

### 4.16 Performed intimacy

Flag `we all know`, `you may feel`, `let us imagine`, `consider for a moment`, and claims about the reader's inner state.

Invite without conscripting. The narrator may address the reader, but should not tell the reader what they already believe or feel.

### 4.17 Dramatic fragments

Flag isolated fragments and one-sentence paragraphs that repeatedly simulate revelation.

Keep them when syntax, silence, or shock carries real force. Rejoin them when typography is doing the argument's work.

### 4.18 Overexplained metaphor

Flag metaphors followed by several sentences that translate every element back into abstraction.

Trust a strong image. Explain only the part required for the argument. Remove decorative metaphors that add atmosphere but no perception.

### 4.19 Mechanical cadence

Flag long runs of sentences with similar length, clause count, openings, or endings. Also flag a repeated rhythm of long exposition followed by a tiny dramatic verdict.

Vary rhythm according to attention and emphasis. Read the passage aloud. If every sentence asks for the same breath, inspect the structure.

### 4.20 Lexical monoculture

Flag nearby repetition of favored abstractions and prestige terms. In these manuscripts, common domain terms include `coherence`, `architecture`, `intelligence`, `relation`, `substrate`, `civilization`, `emergence`, `regenerative`, and `stewardship`.

Do not purge domain vocabulary. Ask whether each recurrence names the exact concept, conceals a more concrete statement, or merely echoes the project's lexicon.

### 4.21 Empty intensifiers

Flag `very`, `deeply`, `truly`, `genuinely`, `remarkably`, `extraordinarily`, `incredibly`, and similar adverbs when they substitute for evidence or exact description.

Delete, specify, or earn the intensity.

### 4.22 Exhaustive pairings

Flag repeated constructions such as `both X and Y`, `whether X or Y`, and `from X to Y` when they imply completeness without establishing it.

Use the pairing when its bounds matter. Otherwise name the relevant cases plainly.

### 4.23 Meta claims about the text

Flag `this chapter argues`, `as we have seen`, `what follows`, `the previous section established`, and `this book will show` when navigation can be simpler or the claim can be stated directly.

Keep genuine wayfinding in a long philosophical work. Remove ceremonial announcements of work the prose has not yet done.

### 4.24 Gloss without substance

Flag sentences that sound elegant but cannot be paraphrased into a clear proposition, image, question, or emotional movement.

Ask what the sentence commits the work to. If the answer is nothing, remove it or discover the missing thought.

## 5. Philosophical prose standard

### Make the argument visible

Identify premises, inferences, definitions, examples, counterarguments, qualifications, and conclusions. A reader should not need to mistake confidence for logic.

### Protect scope and modality

Preserve distinctions among `is`, `often is`, `can be`, `may become`, `should be`, and `would be if`. These small words carry much of a philosophical argument's honesty.

### Distinguish levels

Do not slide without notice among individuals, communities, institutions, civilizations, ecosystems, and metaphysical claims. Name the level when a sentence changes it.

### Define terms by use

Keep project terms stable. Prefer a clear operational or experiential distinction over a sequence of near-synonyms. Do not make a key term mean whatever the current paragraph needs.

### Earn universals

Inspect `all`, `every`, `none`, `never`, `always`, `inevitable`, and `the only`. Replace them when the argument supports only a tendency or bounded claim.

### Respect counterarguments

Present the strongest plausible objection. Do not construct a foolish opponent for rhetorical convenience. A reply should narrow, deepen, or support the thesis.

### Separate vision from report

Mark proposals, hopes, predictions, and invitations as such. Do not describe Providence or another future institution in the present tense when the passage could be mistaken for a report about existing implementation.

## 6. Poetic and narrative standard

### Preserve productive ambiguity

An image may hold more than one meaning. Clarify only when ambiguity blocks the intended experience or creates a false factual implication.

### Protect the image's life

Do not replace a strange, exact image with a clean abstraction. Do not add an abstraction that tells the reader what a sufficient image already shows.

### Preserve earned repetition

Repetition may act as incantation, return, grief, insistence, or structural memory. Keep it when the recurrence changes the reader. Cut it when it merely repeats information.

### Read for breath

Read poems, invocations, blessings, and high-register prose aloud. Honor pauses created by lineation and syntax. Fix breathlessness that arises from accidental clause stacking.

### Resist prestige diction

Prefer the inevitable word to the impressive word. A rare word should be more exact, more musical, or more alive than its ordinary alternative.

### Do not normalize every register

The analytical, narrative, prophetic, intimate, comic, and liturgical passages may sound different. Consistency of intelligence matters more than consistency of surface tone.

## 7. Sentence-level method

Give every sentence one disposition:

| Disposition | Use it when |
| --- | --- |
| Keep | Meaning, rhythm, and placement already work |
| Tighten | The sentence contains removable weight but sound structure |
| Recast | The thought is sound but syntax, focus, or diction fails it |
| Split | Distinct thoughts compete inside one sentence |
| Merge | Separate sentences fragment one movement without benefit |
| Move | The sentence belongs elsewhere in the paragraph or section |
| Query | Intent, source, referent, or claim cannot be inferred safely |
| Remove | The sentence is redundant, empty, misleading, or harmful to the passage |

For every changed sentence, test:

1. What proposition, image, question, or movement did the original contain?
2. What changed in subject, verb, object, scope, modality, time, causality, or emphasis?
3. What was removed, and was it redundant or meaningful?
4. Does the revision sound like this volume?
5. Can the sentence be understood on one attentive reading?
6. Does it need its current length?
7. Does its rhythm support its meaning?
8. Does any phrase sound imported from a generic essay?

## 8. Paragraph and section method

### Paragraph test

Each paragraph should have a discernible center and movement. Identify what changes between its first and final sentence. If nothing changes, inspect for restatement. If several unrelated things change, split or reorder.

### Transition test

Name the actual relation between paragraphs. Preserve a transition only if the relation would otherwise be unclear. Avoid decorating a gap with a generic connector.

### Section test

Each section should make a distinct contribution to the volume. Check whether its opening creates a real question or pressure, whether its middle advances it, and whether its ending resolves, complicates, or deliberately carries it forward.

### Heading and identity test

Treat a heading as editorial language. Improve it when it is vague, formulaic, inflated, redundant, misleading, or inherited from an inferior generation pass. Section, chapter, and part identities may change with it. Do not preserve a weak heading to protect a slug.

Before accepting the change, confirm that every former route resolves through the route ledger and alias system. A link may retain history without dictating current language.

### Neighbor test

Read the end of the preceding section and the beginning of the following section. Remove repeated recaps and introductions that become visible only at the boundary.

## 9. Fact, quotation, and citation handling

- Preserve quotation text exactly unless checking it against the primary source.
- Preserve citation links and identifiers during prose edits.
- Verify names, titles, dates, numbers, study claims, historical claims, and attributed positions when fact checking is in scope.
- Prefer primary sources and original scholarship.
- Record the source for every factual correction.
- Do not invent a citation to rescue a sentence.
- Mark unsupported claims for author review when research is outside scope.
- Distinguish a scholar's claim from the manuscript's extension of that claim.
- Keep unresolved citation markers, including `[verify]`, visibly unresolved until research closes them. Editorial polish is not scholarly verification.

## 10. Acceptance checklist

A reviewed batch is complete only when all answers are yes:

- Was every sentence given an editorial disposition?
- Are all changed claims semantically faithful or explicitly approved?
- Are argument, evidence, inference, analogy, and aspiration distinguishable?
- Are factual corrections sourced?
- Are quotations, citations, names, and numbers intact or verified?
- Does the revision retain the volume's characteristic voice?
- Did independent review look for semantic drift and literary flattening?
- Are canned transitions, false contrasts, generic uplift, and redundant conclusions removed where they were defects?
- Are abstractions grounded where the passage permits grounding?
- Does sentence and paragraph rhythm vary with meaning?
- Are em dashes, en dashes, and two-hyphen prose punctuation absent?
- Are headings, section boundaries, Markdown, routes, aliases, and generated data valid?
- Do all historical section, chapter, and part routes resolve after any heading or identity change?
- Was audio impact checked when source text changed?
- Does the pull request disclose unresolved questions and residual risks?
